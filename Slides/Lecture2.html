A subgraph $S$ of $\Gamma$ is a graph $S$ where

  • $V(S)\subset V(\Gamma)$
  • $E(S)\subset E(\Gamma)$

If an edge $e$ is in $S$, then we need both its ends in $S$.

Isomorphisms

When are two graphs

"the same"?

Definition, slightly loose

An isomorphism of graphs is a bijection between the vertices that preserves the number of edges between pairs of vertices.

Definition, more detailed

An isomorphism from a simple graph $G$ to a graph $H$ is a bijection $$\varphi:V(G)\to V(H)$$ so that, for any $v,w\in V(G)$, the number of edges between $v$ and $w$ (in $G$) is the same as the number of edges between $\varphi(v)$ and $\varphi(w)$ (in $H$).

The isomorphism problem:

Given two graphs $G$ and $H$, either find an isomorphism $\varphi:G\to H$, or prove such an isomorphism doesn't exist.

You need to be be able to answer instances of the isomorphism problem for the exam

A little mathematical culture

The isomorphism problem is of fundamental importance to theoretical computer science.
  • Practical applications
  • It's in NP, but not known to be NP complete or in P

Mathematical culture, continued

P vs NP: complexity theory

  • A Yes/No question is in P if it can be solved in time polynomial in the input size
  • A Yes/No question is in NP if the answer is Yes, I can give you some information that lets you check that in polynomial time.
  • Settling whether or not P=NP is worth a million dollars (at least)

Mathematical culture, continued

In November 2015, Laszlo Babai announced a quasipolynomial-time algorithm for the graph isomorphism problem. The proof that his algorithm is correct depends on the classification of finite simple groups, a deep result whose proof is several thousand pages long. His work is still actively being checked; in January 2017 he patched a hole that had been found.

Why group theory?

Definition

Let $\textrm{Aut}(\Gamma)$ be the set of all isomorphisms $\varphi:G\to G$. Then $G$ is a group.

Theorem

Any finite group $H$ appears is of the form $\text{Aut}(\Gamma)$ for some finite graph $\Gamma$.

Idea of proof

  • There's a natural way to build a graph (the Cayley graph) from a group $H$ and a set of generators of $H$.
  • $H<\textrm{Aut}(\Gamma)$, and can modify the construction to get equality

Ok, but how do I find an isomorphism?

One useful method -- expanding local isomorphisms.
  • An isomorphism $\varphi:G\to H$would have to preserve the degree, so pick one vertex $v$; it could map to any vertex $w\in H$ with $d(v)=d(w)$
  • Now, the vertices adjance to $v$ would have to map to the vertices adjacent to $w$; they have to match degrees, etc...
  • Continue expanding $\varphi$ out from $v$

How to prove two graphs aren't isomorphic?

Could do a case-by-case: expand out as above, and show it's not possible. Usually better to find an obstruction -- a difference between the two graphs
